I grew up in Juneau, which is a small town in Southeast Alaska.


Later I went to school at Carnegie Mellon University, where I studied physics. During summers I worked at the Sears Dealer Store my parents owned. I started with sales and deliveries and later moved to managing the sales and delivery staff. I also installed an electronic time-clock and internet accesible security cameras.


From there I went to work at the US Patent Office. I worked in the Measuring and Testing art unit (2856 represent!), which covers a pretty wide variety of devices and techniques, some more interesting than others. My personal favorite patent application that I worked on was US 7,484,408, for being a relatively clever way of measuring a foot's volume and using fewer materials than other similar devices. My least favorite patent that I worked on was US 7,603,901, which is basically a teapot with a spirit level. I was not personally impressed.


I decided that I really wasn't cut out for patent examining, because my tedium tolerance is too low and my passion for measuring devices is not sufficiently high (although I like measuring devices more than your average man on the street). I left in the summer of '09 to program for NOAA in my hometown.


At NOAA, I principally work on the eLandings project, although I sometimes fill in for internal projects on the Alaska Region's Catch Accounting System. Elandings is a reporting software used by commercial fisherman to tell NOAA and the Alaska Department of Fish and Game how many fish they've caught and/or processed. I work on the web interface, the server side, database portions and a Java Swing client. Most of what I work on is concentrated in the logbook and groundfish reporting portions of the system.
